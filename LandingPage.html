en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning
Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence focused on building systems that learn from data. At its core, most models rely on gradient descent to minimize a loss function and iteratively update parameters.

The update rule is expressed as: θ ← θ − α∇L(θ)

This process repeats across thousands of training steps, allowing the model to learn complex mappings between inputs and outputs — forming the foundation of modern deep learning.

Explaining: Backpropagation

Intermediate

🧠 Imagine you're learning to shoot basketball free-throws. Every miss, your coach tells you to adjust — a little left, more power. Over thousands of shots, you get better. Backprop is the "coach" for AI.

🌟 In plain words: The network looks at how wrong it was, then nudges every number inside itself to be a little less wrong next time.

Backpropagation computes gradients of the loss function with respect to each weight using the chain rule. These gradients tell us how to adjust weights to reduce error.

∂L/∂w = ∂L/∂a · ∂a/∂z · ∂z/∂w

Weights are updated in the direction that decreases loss, scaled by learning rate α.

Backprop applies reverse-mode automatic differentiation through the computational graph. Each node stores its local Jacobian; gradients propagate backward via chain rule accumulation.

δˡ = ((Wˡ⁺¹)ᵀ δˡ⁺¹) ⊙ σ'(zˡ)

Vanishing gradients addressed via ReLU activations, residual connections, and gradient clipping.
